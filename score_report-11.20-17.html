WHAT IS
PT Scoretm?

PT Score™ is a risk Management Model, designed to identify, Measure, & Track Significant Risk Trends in depository Financial institutions.

PT Score™ IS CALCULATED FROM YOUR INSTITUTION’S RISK PROFILE IN FIVE COMPONENT CATEGORIES:


PT Score™ Overview

PT Score™ utilizes your Call Report data to generate a single overall risk score. The score is derived from 50 correlated metrics that are built around the regulatory CAMELS rating structure. The normalized point range falls between zero and 500. A PT Score™ will increase as institutions take on greater levels of risk. A PT Score™ in excess of 300 is generally associated with an unsatisfactory risk position.

PT Score™ Model


How does PT Score™ help my institution?

PT Score™ helps you to understand and manage your institution’s ongoing risk profile by providing you with an ongoing picture of:

  • Your structural risk position
  • Your regulatory risk position
  • Your risk position relative to peers
  • Your risk position relative to the entire industry
  • Early indications (advance warnings) of adverse risk positions

PT Score™ helps institutions optimize their risk position and create greater long-term value for their organization. Through an ongoing and objective assessment of risk, bank managers can pursue more profitable and advantageous business opportunities with greater clarity and confidence.

PT Score™ Overview

The PT Score™ rating model assigns individual component risk scores based on the regulatory CAMELS rating structure. Separate scores are assigned to the specific component categories described below. These component scores are generated from the same correlated risk metrics that are used to create your total PT Score.™

PT Score™ component ratings will not always align precisely with regulatory component ratings. However, a high or elevated PT Score™ will most often be associated with an adverse rating.

Capital Risk Score explanation

The capital risk score is intended to capture the level, trend and risk insulating capability of an institution’s capital base. Higher primary capital ratios generally reflect lower risks and receive lower point allocations. However, risk points may be added based on negative risk factors such weak asset quality, extraordinarily high growth levels, and concerns with liquidity and/or interest rate sensitivity.
